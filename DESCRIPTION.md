# The m · 2^n Accumulation Mystery

This repository is dedicated to a fascinating mathematical observation that can be easily reproduced using any random number generator.

The core of the problem is that a random cumulative sequence somehow "picks favorites" among mathematical families, and these preferences remain indefinitely stable.

To investigate this, we built **nums** — a cumulative-sum structure explorer. Included in this repository, `nums` is an interactive web app designed to probe the hidden statistical structure of long digit sequences (like the digits of pi or any random stream). It turns the theoretical questions below into live tables, evolution charts, and convergence metrics. You can load any dataset, change the accumulation rules, and watch in real-time as the law of large numbers separates true signal from the mere *appearance* of structure.

To grasp the essence of the phenomenon, choose your preferred difficulty level.

## Level 1: Simple (Pen-and-Paper Experiment)

Imagine rolling a 10-sided die (numbers 0 to 9) and recording the cumulative sum of your rolls.

- Rolled 1, sum = **1**
- Rolled 4, sum = 1 + 4 = **5**
- Rolled 6, sum = 5 + 6 = **11**
- Rolled 3, sum = 11 + 3 = **14**

We get the cumulative sequence: `{1, 5, 11, 14, 19, 26, 28...}`

Now, let's pick any odd starting number (let's call it the base m). For example, m = 5. We will multiply it by 2, generating its own personal "family":

- Family 5: `{5, 10, 20, 40, 80...}`
- Family 21: `{21, 42, 84, 168...}`

**The Mystery:** If we check how often our random sequence "lands" on numbers from different families, we witness a paradox. We hit Family 5 in **~16%** of cases. But Family 21 is hit in only **~7%** of cases. And even if we roll the die a million times, these percentages will never equalize! Every odd number has its own "innate" match percentage. Why does a random process show such bias toward different numbers?

## Level 2: Intermediate (For Programmers)

Any developer can verify this phenomenon in a few minutes by writing a simple script, or by exploring the `nums` app interface.

**Verification Algorithm:**

1. Generate an array of 1,000,000 random integers in the range [0, 9].
2. Convert it into an array of prefix sums (the cumulative sequence). The maximum number will be around 4,500,000.
3. Take a set of odd numbers m (e.g., 5, 13, 21, 49).
4. For each m, generate an array of values m * 2^n until the values exceed the maximum of our cumulative sequence.
5. Calculate the `Exact Match %` — the percentage of numbers from the m * 2^n family that are strictly present in the prefix sum array.

**Expected Result (Theory):**
Since the average step from 0 to 9 is 4.5, according to the laws of statistics, the probability of landing on any arbitrarily large number is 1 / 4.5 = **22.22%**.

**Actual Result (Anomaly):**
  m = 5  -> Exact Match ~16%
  m = 21 -> Exact Match ~7%
  m = 49 -> Exact Match ~19%

When increasing the sample size to 10 or 100 million numbers, these percentages "freeze" and form straight horizontal lines on graphs. They do not converge to the expected **22.22%**. Question: Where do these individual constants come from?

## Level 3: Advanced (For Mathematicians)

The phenomenon lies at the intersection of probability theory, random walk theory, and 2-adic dynamics.

Let S_k = \sum_{i=1}^k x_i be the partial sums of independent random variables x_i.

**Why don't classical theorems apply directly here?**
If we were investigating a simple arithmetic progression (the set of multiples m * n), the question would reduce to Markov chains and modular arithmetic: S_k \equiv 0 \pmod m. For such sets, local ergodic convergence applies, and the probability of a visit approaches 1/m. The family has a density of 1/m among natural numbers.

However, in our case, the family is defined as F_m = { m * 2^n | n in N }.
The density of this set is **zero**. It is infinite but exponentially sparse. For such sets, the general theory of modular distribution is weaker, and subtle structural properties come into play.

**Current Hypotheses for the Origins of Constants C(m):**

1. **Logarithmic Sampling Effect:** Due to the m * 2^n structure, the number of checks grows as \log_2(X). Even with massive datasets, the denominator barely exceeds 30–50 elements. The initial variance might permanently lock the cumulative average, creating an illusion of convergence.
2. **Arithmetic (2-adic) Resonance:** The steps x_i in [0, 9] do not have a uniform distribution modulo 2^k (for instance, modulo 4 exhibits probability shifts). The random walk interferes with the 2-adic structure of the core m, creating a unique probability density for each subset.
